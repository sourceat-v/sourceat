The Grandma Cookie Revival

A Cookie From the Goryeo Dynasty

Yakgwa (약과) has been part of Korean food culture for over 700 years. The name literally means "medicine confection" — 약 (yak, medicine) + 과 (gwa, confection) — because honey and ginger were considered medicinal in traditional Korean food philosophy. It was a luxury item, served at royal courts and major ceremonies: weddings, ancestral rites, seasonal festivals.

The process is precise. Wheat flour is kneaded with sesame oil, ginger juice, and rice wine — not water, which would develop gluten and ruin the texture. The dough is pressed into flower-shaped molds, deep-fried slowly in oil at low temperature until golden, then submerged in a warm honey-ginger-cinnamon syrup until saturated. The result is soft, dense, fragrant, and deeply sweet in a way that feels ancient.

For much of the 20th century, it was old-fashioned. Younger Koreans associated it with their grandmothers' kitchens and ceremonial food spread out for ancestral rites — meaningful, but not cool.

How Gen Z Flipped the Script

Around 2022, something shifted. The trend had a name before anyone fully understood it: 할매니얼 (halmaenial) — 할머니 (grandmother) + millennial. Korean Gen Z began treating grandmother-era foods not as something to move past, but as something worth rediscovering. Yakgwa was at the center of it.

Specialty yakgwa shops opened in Hongdae, Seongsu, and Mangwon. Lines stretched around the block. Cafes started serving yakgwa with coffee — the bittersweet espresso against the honey-ginger sweetness turned out to be a genuinely good pairing. Instagram filled with flat-lay photos of flower-shaped cookies on traditional pojagi cloth. The aesthetic was deliberate: traditional Korean visual culture, but through a contemporary lens.

The halmaenial wave wasn't just nostalgia. It was a confident assertion that Korean food culture didn't need to chase Western trends — that it had something entirely its own worth celebrating. Yakgwa became a symbol of that.

The Flavor No One Can Quite Describe

Every American who tries yakgwa for the first time struggles to place it. It's not a donut — there's no yeast, no lightness. It's not a cookie — the texture is too dense and moist. It's not quite a candy — it's savory at its base. It resists every Western category you try to put it in.

The sesame oil makes the dough rich and faintly nutty. The ginger gives a warm, subtle heat that builds slowly. The cinnamon adds depth without dominating. The honey syrup soaks all the way through — you can't separate the cookie from the glaze. And it lingers, in the best way.

The closest comparison most people land on: "If baklava and a soft caramel had a child raised in Korea." But even that doesn't quite capture it.

Beyond Yakgwa — The Full Hangwa Family

Yakgwa is one product in a broader category called 한과 (hangwa) — traditional Korean confections. Its siblings are worth knowing. 매작과 (maejakgwa) are fried ribbon cookies — crunchier, less sweet, twisted into a knot shape. 강정 (gangjeong) are puffed rice or sesame clusters bound with honey syrup — lighter and crunchy. 인절미 (injeolmi) are chewy glutinous rice cakes coated in roasted soybean flour — earthy, nutty, pillowy.

The modern derivatives are also worth trying. 약과맛 아몬드 (yakgwa-flavored almonds) took the honey-ginger glaze and applied it to almonds — the product that introduced the flavor profile to people who'd never heard of yakgwa. 흑임자 약과 (black sesame yakgwa) merges the halmaenial trend with the black sesame boom, adding a rich, roasted depth to the original recipe.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does yakgwa taste like?

Dense, moist dough deep-fried and saturated with honey-ginger-cinnamon syrup. The sesame oil in the dough adds a faint nuttiness; the ginger gives warm, subtle heat. Not crunchy — closer to a soft caramel in texture. Sweet but balanced. Most Americans describe it as unlike anything they've had before.

Why did yakgwa go viral in Korea?

The 할매니얼 (halmaenial) trend — Korean Gen Z reclaiming their grandmothers' foods. Specialty yakgwa shops with hour-long queues opened across Seoul starting 2022. Part nostalgia, part cultural pride, part genuine discovery that these flavors are excellent. TikTok spread it beyond Korea.

How do you eat yakgwa?

Straight from the bag, at room temperature. Koreans often pair it with barley tea or sikhye (sweet rice drink), which cuts the sweetness. It also pairs surprisingly well with espresso — the bitterness balances the honey. Don't refrigerate: the texture firms up and loses the moist, dense quality that makes it good.

What's the difference between packaged and fresh yakgwa?

Packaged yakgwa (Chungwoo, Crown, Haitai) is good — well-made, consistent, and widely available. Fresh yakgwa from a specialty shop is in another category: more honey-saturated, softer, often made with better-quality sesame oil and real ginger juice. If you're in a city with Korean bakeries, try to find fresh first.

What is hangwa (한과)?

Hangwa is the category name for traditional Korean confections — the full family that includes yakgwa, maejakgwa (fried ribbon cookies), gangjeong (puffed rice candy), jeonggwa (candied fruit/vegetables), and injeolmi (soybean flour rice cakes). Yakgwa is the most famous member but not the only one worth trying.

Is yakgwa gluten-free or vegan?

No to gluten-free — it's made with wheat flour. Vegan status depends on the brand: traditional yakgwa uses no animal products (wheat flour, sesame oil, honey), but honey is excluded from strict vegan diets. Check specific brand ingredients for any additional components.
